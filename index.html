II · Narration 🌿
The story of our school garden
a. Preparation of the Garden
Issues & Concerns

The garden plot in front of the Science Building was in rough shape when we started. The soil was hard, dry, and had a lot of trash and weeds, making it really difficult to work with. We also lacked basic tools like shovels and watering cans, so we had to borrow sometimes from the Science Lab. One of the hardest parts was actually transplanting the seedlings, the soil was still too compacted to properly receive new roots, and several plants did not survive the transfer. I personally grew one plant at home and brought it to the garden, but unfortunately it did not make it. Suspended classes also made it difficult to consistently coordinate garden preparation activities. At times, participation and communication within the class were inconsistent, and without a clearly assigned duty schedule, responsibilities were not always evenly distributed.

Solutions & Recommendations

We pushed through by working with whatever we had. We cleared the area, tilled the soil, removed weeds, and added rice husk as organic fertilizer to improve soil quality. Classmates brought plants, recyclable containers, and tools from home, and we divided tasks so each group had a specific responsibility. For future batches, I recommend improving soil quality weeks before planting begins, hardening seedlings gradually before transplanting, and setting a clear duty schedule with accountability measures from day one. Having a dedicated watering tool stored near the garden would also make a big difference, especially during the early transplanting stage when the plants need the most consistent care.

b. Management of the Garden
Issues & Concerns

Maintaining the garden alive and healthy after planting was just as challenging as setting it up. On online class days, the garden would go unwatered for several days at a time, and some of the younger plants could not survive that. The heat during this period was intense, and without regular watering, a few plants dried out before they could fully establish. We also had no dedicated watering tool near the garden, so we constantly had to borrow from the Science Lab or fetch water from the nearby comfort room using small plastic bottles. Other students would throw trash into the plot too, so cleaning became part of our routine on top of everything else. Changing schedules and other school activities, such as the intramurals, also made it challenging to maintain a consistent workflow and balance responsibilities throughout the project.

Solutions & Recommendations

On every face-to-face class day, we made sure to water and check on the plants. As a Student Assistant in the nearby Science Lab, I watered the garden during my duty hours whenever I had free time, which helped fill in the gaps on days when fewer classmates showed up. Some of us also used our group chat to coordinate who could pass by the garden on off days. For future classes, I recommend creating a watering schedule that covers non-class days, storing a water container permanently near the garden, and posting a sign to discourage littering. Clear consequences for absent members would also help make participation more consistent and fair.

c. Harvest / Growth of Plants
Plants Grown
🍆 Eggplant 🌿 Okra 🍅 Tomato 🌶️ Chili 🌸 Orchids 🪴 Foliage Plants

We grew a mix of vegetable crops and ornamental plants. For vegetables, we cultivated eggplant, okra, chili, and tomato, all started at home before being transplanted to the school garden. The eggplants and okra did well and actually produced fruits, while the tomato plant almost produced fruit, but I noticed that it began to rot while it was still small and not yet fully mature, and the chili plants stayed green and healthy but did not flower or fruit within the term. For ornamentals, we planted Chinese evergreen, snake plant, calathea, orchids, and other decorative foliage plants. Our class worked together to organize the garden. Two of my classmates built the plant racks, while others helped with the design and decoration. I created the signages, another classmate laminated them, and others placed the signs on display, making the garden look organized and well put together. Seeing the space transform from a dry, littered lot into something green and structured was one of the most satisfying parts of the whole experience.

Health Benefits

Every plant in our garden carried real value. Eggplant is rich in antioxidants, fiber, and vitamins that support heart health and digestion. Okra provides folate and fiber that help regulate blood sugar and support cardiovascular health. Chili pepper contains capsaicin, which boosts metabolism and strengthens the immune system. Tomato is well known for its lycopene content, which supports heart health. The ornamental plants also contributed meaningfully. Chinese evergreen, snake plant, calathea, orchids, and other decorative foliage plants helped purify air by absorbing pollutants and increasing oxygen levels. Beyond that, having greenery around has real mental health benefits. It reduces stress, improves mood, and makes the environment feel more calm and inviting. Gardening itself, as we experienced firsthand, is also good for overall well-being from physical to mental health.

Environmental Advantages

Our garden did more for the environment than it might appear at first glance. Every plant contributed to cleaner air through photosynthesis, and the organic amendments we used like rice husk improved soil structure and water retention without any chemical inputs. The roots helped stabilize the previously compacted and eroded soil, while the flowering plants attracted pollinators that support biodiversity on campus. Growing food locally, even at this small scale, reduces dependence on commercially transported produce and lowers the carbon footprint that comes with it. This experience made it clear that sustainable practices do not require large resources or formal programs. A school garden, a sack of rice husk, and a group of students willing to show up can already make a difference.

d. Reflections / Insights

This subject gave me a lot more than I expected. Tending the garden, carrying water from heavy bucket to small plastic bottles, pulling out trash others left behind, watching plants slowly grow in soil we prepared ourselves, all of it felt genuinely meaningful. I found a kind of pride in it that surprised me. I learned patience waiting for plants to respond. I learned accountability by showing up even when attendance was low. And I gained a real appreciation for where food actually comes from. Seeing my classmates contribute in their own ways made it even more rewarding. Some designed layouts and built racks, improving the garden's landscape design. Some adjusted watering schedules to beat the heat. Some stayed active in the group chat to keep things coordinated. As a future Science teacher, this reminded me that some of the best lessons happen outside the classroom. In the dirt, under the sun, with your hands doing the actual work. That is the kind of learning I want to bring into my teaching someday. 🌱
